# Lesson 2 — Before the Interface

When I started Viora, I wasn't trying to build a beautiful app.

I wasn't thinking about colors, typography, or animations.

In fact, I didn't even know what the screens should look like.

I only knew one thing: I wanted the app to feel calm. To be simple, airy, free of distracting elements.

I don't like cluttered interfaces, full of colors and animations competing for the user's attention. I wanted the app to let the content speak.

That's when I realized something.

I wasn't looking for a design.

I was looking for a feeling.

Since visual design isn't my strong suit, I turned to AI. Not to build my app for me, but to explore different directions. I described what I imagined the user should feel when opening the app.

AI proposed several options. None of them was exactly what I was looking for, but each had something I liked — a structure, a dashboard, a typography, an organization.

Instead of choosing one of them, I started combining them: I kept the dashboard structure from one option, the airy typography from another, and dropped any color that didn't serve the calm I was looking for. Little by little, Viora built its own identity.

Looking back, I don't think AI designed this app. It helped me explore. But every decision remained mine. Every screen is the result of many small choices, not a single generated image.

We often think design starts with choosing colors. For me, it started with a feeling. The interface came only after that.

I didn't start with a screen.

I started with a feeling.

---

**A separate note, for those interested in the technical side:** I deliberately chose a task management app because I wanted a project simple enough to let me focus on learning SwiftData, without being distracted by unnecessary complexity.
